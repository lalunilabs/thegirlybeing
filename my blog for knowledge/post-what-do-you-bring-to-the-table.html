What Do You Bring to the Table?

If you have to convince someone of your worth, you’re already sitting at the wrong table.

Let me tell you something real — something I’ve been carrying around for too long, and I know I’m not alone in this.

As women, we are constantly, subtly, and so habitually conditioned to prove our worth. We fight a silent battle with the people around us, always searching for that one shot — that moment where if we get it right once and for all, we can prove ourselves to the world, especially to our loved ones.

If only it was that simple.

The Fight We Never Signed Up For

Here’s what happens: when we try to prove ourselves, we end up fighting with the people and things that matter the most.

Instead of trying to explain what we want, what we need, the way we feel — we fight for it. And when we choose to fight, the other person naturally wants to defend. It becomes a never-ending cycle of conflict where nobody wins.

And then comes that haunting thought: If we don’t fight, what do we do? Just let the opportunity pass away? Let people and situations walk all over us?

Showing vs. Proving: The Energy Feels Different

Here’s what I learned — and it changed everything for me:

There’s a massive difference between fighting to prove your worth and showing your worth naturally.

When you’re fighting and dying to prove you’re enough, you’re not just fighting for a thing — you’re fighting for the symbol of your worth. It’s desperate. It’s exhausting. It’s laced with struggle and anger and fear.

But when you show your worth? It’s like quiet assurance. It flows. It’s confident without being defensive. It’s knowing your value so deeply that you don’t need anyone else’s validation to confirm it.

Drop into your body for a second and feel it: proving versus being. The energy is not the same.

— The difference you can feel, even before you can explain

The Question That Doesn’t Make Sense

“What do I, as a woman, bring to the table?”

I never had the answer to this question because I always thought: why am I being questioned about this only in the case of a marriage or partnership? And not friendships, or the mother-child relationship?

Why do I have to show proof of what I bring?

And here’s the uncomfortable truth we need to talk about: we’re being asked to play a game with rules that were never designed for us to win.

Why Are We Comparing Ourselves to Men’s Standards?

We, as women, are built differently. Our strengths exist in different dimensions. So why do we have to prove ourselves to them like it’s a never-ending competition?

Yes, times have changed. And because women decided to speak up (and many women supported them along with a few good men), we got here.

Now we fight for equal pay at work — and also, as silly as it sounds, we fight against people opening the door for us as a nice gesture because “we can do it ourselves.”

But it doesn’t work like that. As a society, we have to coexist. The giving, the nourishing, the receiving — the embracing cycle has to flow.

When we reject every gesture, every acknowledgment of our feminine energy, we’re not proving strength. We’re exhausting ourselves.

The Battle Within Ourselves

I don’t know what’s right or wrong, but I know this: battling within ourselves isn’t doing us any good.

Because before the question of proving our worth to someone even arises, we’ve already estimated our worth. And we’ve probably undervalued it.

When we fight to prove ourselves, we often end up in relationships where we lose ourselves instead of being in healthy connection with others.

We become so focused on meeting their standards that we forget to ask: Do they meet mine?

The Problem With “What Do You Bring to the Table?”

Using that question to undermine someone or establish superiority is wrong. It turns the conversation transactional — like someone with a shopping cart checking what’s on display.

Just because a man can bring things you can see — like a house or a car — doesn’t mean the things you can’t weigh are worth less.

Imagine eating good-looking food and calling it the best you’ve ever had… without tasting it, smelling it, or actually being nourished by it.

The question itself doesn’t make sense.

What We Actually Bring

Do you want to know what women bring to the table that can’t be quantified on a spreadsheet?

  • Emotional intelligence that holds families together through impossible times.
  • Intuition that reads the room — the relationship — the subtle shifts that everyone else misses.
  • The ability to create home: not just a house, but the feeling of safety and warmth that makes it worth coming back to.
  • Nurturing that sees potential in people and helps them grow into their best selves.
  • Beauty, softness, creativity, connection, life itself.

But more than anything? We bring ourselves — our whole, complex, beautiful, messy, powerful selves.

The Truth About Relationships

For a relationship to work, it’s not about one person bringing 60% and the other bringing 40% and constantly measuring who contributes more.

Healthy relationships are about synergy — not competition. You both bring different things that complement each other.

“Why do you need me to bring something to your table? Do you not know how to fill your own table?”

“I have my own table and it’s fantastic.”

“And we can see if we’re aligned and we want to push our tables together.”

— The reframe that ends the performance

The Real Question

So when someone asks you, “What do you bring to the table?” maybe the real question you should ask yourself is:

Is this even my table? Do I want to sit here?

Because if you have to convince someone of your worth, you’re already sitting at the wrong table.

The right person won’t need to ask. They’ll be grateful you chose to sit with them at all.

My POV

I think the most dangerous part of the "table" question is that it quietly trains women to audition for love.

And I don't want your life to feel like an interview. I want it to feel like alignment.

Your job isn't to prove you're valuable. Your job is to build a life so solid, so self-respecting, so full — that any relationship you choose is an addition, not a rescue.
